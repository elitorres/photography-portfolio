About the Photographer

Photographer's Statement

City Through My Lens

In this series, I turn my camera toward the city not as a backdrop, but as a living subject. Through 15 photographs captured on an iPhone, I document the textures, rhythms, and quiet drama of urban life — the streets, bridges, waterways, and corners that make a city breathe. This project is my attempt to slow down and truly see the environment most of us move through without pausing.

My artistic vision is rooted in the belief that extraordinary images do not require extraordinary equipment — they require attention. I am drawn to the warm, golden quality of city light at dusk and dawn, the way it transforms concrete and glass into something almost organic. The inspiration for this work came from a simple observation: that the city is in constant conversation with itself, and most of us are too busy to listen. I wanted to document that conversation.

The central themes of this project are presence, impermanence, and the human imprint. Every image asks: what does it mean to inhabit a space? A deserted street at dusk, a canal reflecting the last light of day, a yellow cab frozen mid-motion — these are not just scenes, they are evidence of lives being lived. I am equally drawn to the overlooked — a garden tucked between towers, wildflowers growing through pavement cracks — as I am to the grand scale of skylines and bridges. Together, they tell a fuller story of what a city is.

Shooting on an iPhone was a deliberate choice. It keeps me light, present, and inconspicuous — a participant in the city rather than an observer from a distance. I rely heavily on natural light, particularly the golden hour, which wraps urban environments in warmth and depth that no filter can replicate. My process is instinctive: I walk, I notice, I shoot. I look for the moment when geometry and light align to create something that feels both spontaneous and inevitable.

I made this work for anyone who has ever walked through a city and felt something — without knowing exactly what. These images are an invitation to look again at the spaces we take for granted, to find beauty in the ordinary, and to recognize that the urban landscape is not a backdrop to human life — it is part of it. I hope these photographs give the viewer permission to slow down, if only for a moment.

Cities are always changing. Streets get repaved, buildings come down, new ones go up. What I have captured here is a specific moment in time — a record of what these places looked and felt like when I stood in them. It is my hope that this work endures as both a personal document and a shared one: a reminder that beauty is everywhere, if you are willing to look for it.
